It was an early morning in October and Dr. Najilla Azizi had just clocked in for her shift at Anabah Maternity Center after a treacherous two-hour journey from the capital city Kabul on a thin mountain road into Afghanistan’s Panjshir Valley.

Azizi, 30 years old and illustrated above, specializes in gynecology in a country where women are only 19 percent of the paid labor force. She works at one of the few maternity hospitals in Afghanistan — and the only free facility in the area. Opened in 2003 by the Italian nonprofit Emergency, Anabah has a rolling team of international medics who support the hospital, but the vast majority of staff — doctors, surgeons, midwives, nurses, and administrators — are Afghans. The maternity wing is entirely staffed by women. Since opening, they have treated over 226,000 women and helped birth 38,000 children.

Afghanistan’s maternal mortality is almost double the global average, making it one of the worst places in the world to give birth. More recent research suggests an even more dire scenario; maternal deaths may be three times as high as originally thought. The country also has one of the world’s highest fertility rates, with an average of five babies born per woman. Girls are expected to start families very young, often when they are still children themselves, and continue giving birth for as long as they can.

“Our culture makes women never want to be examined by a male doctor,” says Azizi. “In many areas, male relatives refuse to bring their wives and daughters to hospital because women cannot be seen by outsiders. So these women die giving birth at home.”

The maternity ward has a strict ‘no men’ policy. Only in extreme circumstances — when the mother or the child’s life is at risk — will the family be asked to give consent for a male surgeon to operate.

Azizi, pictured above, lives with her parents in Kabul and travels for three days at a time to Panjshir for her shifts at the hospital — which is also recognized by the Afghan government as a training hospital for doctors specializing in gynecology and obstetrics.

Azizi’s mother is a midwife and her father an engineer, and they both encouraged her to become a doctor. “[At Anabah], they really allow us to open our brains and we can speak freely,” she says. “The teaching is very practical, we are allowed to train in the operating theatre. And there is very good care for women by women staff. It is respecting Afghan women and treatment is free.”

As Azizi prepared for her morning rounds, a battered car pulled into the hospital compound carrying a young woman who had delivered her baby only moments earlier — in the back seat of the car.

There was no clinic near her village and relatives couldn’t move the woman when she went into labor the night before because the army only secures the roads in daylight.

The war in Afghanistan has raged, mostly unabated, since the U.S. invaded in 2001. Ostensibly an effort to capture Osama bin Laden and disarm the Taliban regime hiding him after the terrorist attacks of September 11, Afghanistan has spiraled to become the longest war in American history.

Less than half of Afghanistan’s territory lies under government control, and most of these areas are urban hubs. Beyond them, vast rural swaths remain under the control of the Taliban. The self-declared Islamic State has also built a presence and Afghan security officials fear this may increase as IS loses territory and power in Syria.

It is in these areas that millions of women and children live cut off from all medical care.

NNoorjan, whose surgery is depicted above, says she couldn’t get to medical treatment because of IS. She is around 48 years old and like some Afghans, only has one name. She traveled to Anabah with her son on horseback over the mountains from Parian district. She was scheduled to have a full hysterectomy at Anabah last September, but said IS invaded her area and the road became too dangerous. It took her a year to find her way back to Anabah. She is in constant pain.

“More and more women are hearing about us and coming to our hospital for treatment, but the conflict means some regions and roads are blocked,” says Mahbina Jawad, a self-proclaimed ‘superstar midwife’ who has worked at Anabah for 14 years.

“Women must travel with a male relative and they are afraid the man may be kidnapped or killed by Daesh or government soldiers,” she says, referring to IS by its Arabic name. “There are also bombs on the road. So either the woman doesn’t make it to hospital at all, or she comes so late the damage is too bad. Often the baby dies and we have to give the woman a hysterectomy so any future pregnancy is finished.”

For the women who make it for treatment, Anabah is a sanctuary. It was founded as a war trauma clinic in the late 1990s by a group of Italian medics who trekked into the remote Panjshir Valley, around 60 miles north of Kabul.

In 2003, just two years after the U.S. invasion, a maternity ward was launched and the hospital prioritized recruiting and training Afghan female medical staff, recognizing the importance in a deeply conservative culture of providing care for women, by women.

Maternal mortality is drastically worse in Afghanistan’s rural areas, in some places four times as high as the capital city.

Women who deliver at home must rely on female relatives to assist them. For women who can’t make it to Anabah, the hospital supports tiny medical posts in a several remote villages around the entrance to the Panjshir Valley. Still, these posts can only treat basic ailments and the hospital itself remains a critical option for women in rural areas.

But care at Anabah is so good that women even travel from Kabul to give birth there.

The hospital isn’t just a sanctuary for the patients; it is for many of the female staff as well.

In her thirties and unmarried — relatively uncommon for Afghan women — Mahbina Jawad, pictured above with her colleague Mariam, says she is devoted to her job. “Suitors come for me but I don’t like them. I have too many demands. I am most happy [working] in the hospital.”

Jawad and Mariam have worked together in maternity since 2003. Jawad was born in Pakistan, where her family fled during the Russian occupation of Afghanistan. She was a school teacher, but was recruited and trained at Anabah and now leads the midwifery unit.

Meanwhile, Azizi and her colleagues rushed the woman and her newborn from the battered car and in for examination. Fortunately, they found the pair in good health. Many mothers across Afghanistan are not so lucky.

Dr. Khadra Wafa, another Afghan female doctor at the hospital, used an ultrasound to examine Saed Khanom, depicted above. Khanoum is 36 years old and pregnant with twins. She is a high-risk patient with diabetes who has miscarried three times and lost four babies in infancy. She says she came to Anabah because her sister-in-law delivered here safely and the hospital has the best reputation.

Bibi Zelima, pictured above, is also a patient at the hospital. She was admitted immediately after arriving by rented vehicle, over the mountains from neighboring Kapisa province. She has given birth to 10 babies at home and four have died in infancy. At a local clinic, she learned her current unborn child was in breech position (feet first) and that she needed specialist help, so she travelled to Anabah. She had never left her village before, and arrived at the hospital in extreme pain before going into labor. “For many women,” says Azizi, “this is the first time they have been outside of their family. That they have seen people who are not relatives.”

As Azizi’s shift comes to a close, her final task of the day is an emergency cesarean section. The mother suffered a cord prolapse in labor, meaning the umbilical cord slipped below the baby in her uterus and as the baby moved into position for birth the cord became trapped between the his head and the mother’s vaginal wall, causing suffocation. According to Italian maternity coordinator Eleonora Bruni, it is impossible to predict whether this is likely to happen until it does. The mother was rushed into surgery but despite efforts of the nurses to resuscitate her child, the baby boy never breathed. The boy had a twin, who had died early on in the pregnancy.

IInsecurity in Afghanistan continues to worsen. This year, the number of civilian deaths reached a record high. Uncounted are the thousands of women unable to reach medical care due to the violence.

In October, the International Committee of the Red Cross was forced to drastically reduce its presence in Afghanistan due to rising insecurity and the deaths and kidnapping of several staff. As concerns for international humanitarian staff in Afghanistan grow, medical options decrease. As options for Afghan women diminish, a small hospital in the foothills, staffed primarily by other Afghan women, grows in importance.
